History

Polanco, a hallmark of Mexico City

The history of the Polanco area began during colonial times, in the 16th century. The Hacienda de San Juan de los Morales was the first building in the area that the King of Spain donated to Hernan Cortes, and it takes its name from the mulberry trees that were planted here.

Around 1930, the lands were divided in response to the demand for urban areas in this region. The population had grown following the Mexican Revolution, due to families wishing to move away from the central area of the city.

The avenue that was originally called Paseo del Emperador, designed as a direct path to transfer Emperor Maximilian I of Habsburg from the Chapultepec Castle to the National Palace, is currently known as Paseo de la Reforma. It was extended out to the Chapultepec Forest and the newly subdivided lands of the Hacienda de los Morales.

This new area, known as Polanco, was populated by upper-middle-class families. Its streets were named after humanists, philosophers and writers.

Cultural diversity has been a hallmark of the Polanco neighborhood. To this day, it continues to be the favorite area for new inhabitants arriving from other countries. They delight in the cosmopolitan vibe and its streets, so full of life.
